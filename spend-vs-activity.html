The HESCET Dilemma

The Higher Education Supply Chain Emissions Tool (HESCET) has been the standard, but its spend-based methodology creates a fundamental paradox that can penalize sustainable choices.

The "Spend Paradox"

HESCET converts financial spend into emissions using industry-average factors ($kgCO_2e/£$). This means if you choose a more expensive, higher-quality, and more sustainable product, your calculated carbon footprint actually goes up.

  • Penalizes Quality: Choosing durable, longer-lasting items can increase your reported emissions due to higher cost.
  • Ignores Supplier Effort: It cannot differentiate between a supplier using renewable energy and one using coal power.
  • Action vs. Accounting: The only reliable way to lower emissions under this model is to simply spend less, not necessarily buy better.

The Rise of Alternatives

Frustration with HESCET has led to new tools. Each represents a step up the data quality ladder, but comes with its own trade-offs.

HESCET / APUC

Spend-BasedTop-Down

Estimates emissions by multiplying financial spend by industry-average emission factors. The APUC tool is a more granular version.

Pros:

  • Low cost and resource-light
  • Good for initial screening & hotspots

Cons:

  • Prone to over/underestimation
  • The "Spend Paradox"
  • Not actionable for suppliers

NetPositive NZC Tool

Supplier-SpecificBottom-Up

Directly engages suppliers to collect their actual Scope 1 & 2 emissions data, or calculates it from their activity data (fuel, electricity).

Pros:

  • Uses "actual" supplier data
  • Avoids the "Spend Paradox"
  • Engages and supports suppliers

Cons:

  • Measures supplier Scope 1 & 2 only
  • Misses supplier's own supply chain (Scope 3)
  • High risk of underestimation vs. GHG Protocol

Advanced EEIO (CEDA)

Advanced Spend-BasedTop-Down

Uses powerful global databases (like CEDA or EXIOBASE) with thousands of highly specific, annually updated emission factors.

Pros:

  • Most accurate top-down method
  • Highly granular and globally aware

Cons:

  • Still an estimation method
  • Requires expert use and can be expensive

The Over/Underestimation Puzzle

The 40-50% variance isn't a simple error. It's a mix of correcting for HESCET's flaws while also potentially introducing new blind spots. Here's what's really happening.

1. Correcting Overestimation

Moving from basic HESCET to a more granular analysis (like LUPC did) reduces the footprint by applying more accurate, lower emission factors to specific goods, avoiding broad-brush averages.

2. Correcting Underestimation

Moving from the old (pre-2021) HESCET to the new version can *increase* the footprint (as GCU found) because the new factors better reflect high-carbon manufacturing in global supply chains.

3. The Boundary Shift Risk

Moving to a tool like NetPositive causes the biggest drop, but it's partly because it doesn't measure the supplier's own supply chain (their Scope 3), which HESCET attempts to estimate. It's a more accurate measurement of a smaller piece of the pie.

Evidence from the Field

How have UK universities navigated this challenge? Their experiences provide a real-world look at the impact of different methodological choices.

Nottingham Trent University: The Pioneer

~50% Reduction

NTU co-developed the NetPositive NZC Supplier Tool to get actual data. By using this primary data for just 18% of their spend, they saw a massive 8,221 tCO2e reduction.

Key Insight: Direct supplier engagement provides the most actionable data and the largest footprint reductions, but requires significant investment in building relationships.

London Universities Purchasing Consortium (LUPC): The Refiner

-64% Reduction

LUPC didn't switch tools, they refined their analysis. By breaking down broad spend categories (e.g., "Professional Services") into specifics (legal, financial, etc.) and applying better factors, they slashed their footprint.

Key Insight: Granularity is key. Significant overestimation is baked into basic HESCET reports. Careful refinement can yield huge accuracy gains.

Imperial College London: The Hybrid Builder

-30,000 tCO2e

Imperial built their own hybrid model. They created 21 new, more relevant spend categories and integrated activity-based data (like actual kWh for energy losses) where possible.

Key Insight: A powerful "middle way" exists. You can significantly improve accuracy by customising spend-based tools to your institution's specific context.

Glasgow Caledonian University: The Global Realist

~100% Increase

GCU's footprint *doubled* when they moved from the old (pre-2021) HESCET to the new version. Why? The updated factors more accurately captured the high carbon intensity of their global supply chain (e.g., electronics from Asia).

Key Insight: Better data doesn't always mean a lower number. The old HESCET was likely under-reporting the impact of globalised manufacturing.

University of Manchester: The Auditor

+14% Increase

Manchester engaged external experts (EcoAct) for a rigorous, GHG Protocol-aligned baseline. The use of more accurate, inflation-adjusted emission factors led to an upward revision of their footprint.

Key Insight: A robust, externally-verified baseline is crucial for credibility, even if it reveals your footprint is larger than previously thought.

The Verdict: A Question of Maturity

So, is HESCET overestimating or are alternatives underestimating? The answer is **both**. The journey to accurate accounting is a progression through levels of data maturity, not a single choice of tool.

1

Level 1: Screening

Use the latest HESCET/APUC tool to get a first-pass footprint. The goal isn't perfect accuracy, but to conduct a Pareto analysis and identify your high-impact "hotspots" (e.g., construction, ICT, labs).

2

Level 2: Refining

Improve your baseline's credibility. Disaggregate the hotspot categories and integrate activity-based data (kWh, mileage, tonnage) where possible, following the Imperial College model.

3

Level 3: Engaging

Shift from estimation to primary data. Use a tool like NetPositive to engage your key hotspot suppliers. Focus your resources where they will have the most material impact on your footprint.

4

Level 4: Integrating

Operate a fully hybrid system. Use the best available top-down estimation for the "long tail" of suppliers, while embedding primary data collection for all strategic suppliers into your core procurement processes and contracts.

Strategic Recommendations

Focus on gathering actionable intelligence, not just chasing the lowest number. The future is hybrid, collaborative, and focused on driving real-world change.

1. Focus on Action, Not the Number

The goal is to find the method that provides the most actionable data. A tool that generates a Carbon Action Plan for a supplier is more valuable than one that just provides a top-down estimate.

2. Embrace a Hybrid Future

No single tool is perfect. The most robust approach combines top-down estimation (for the long tail of suppliers) with bottom-up primary data collection (for your high-impact, strategic partners).

3. Collaborate to Accelerate

This challenge is too big for any one institution. Work through purchasing consortia and sector groups to standardize data requests, share the burden, and send a unified signal to the supply chain.
